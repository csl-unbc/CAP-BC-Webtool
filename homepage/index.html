Project

Climate Adaptive Planning for British Columbia (CAP-BC) is an online, open-access webtool to provide climate–adapted systematic conservation planning across BC. CAP-BC provides a means of adapting our conservation plans to the projected impacts of climate change, thereby ensuring that our protected lands are as resilient as possible moving forward and provide a tool to our partners that promotes the development of a network of protected areas in BC that is adaptive to future climate change

Why?

The impacts of climate change are already being felt in British Columbia. These impacts are projected to diversify and intensify over this century, which poses an unprecedented threat to our socio-economic systems and natural environment. There is an urgent need for solutions- focused research on how to adapt business-as-usual practices to minimize our vulnerability to such risks, particularly as they affect biodiversity conservation, ecosystem services, and ‘at risk’ Species.

The core question we address in partnership with The Nature Trust, Canadian Parks and Wilderness Society and BC Parks Foundation is:

  • How can we adapt our conservation plans in BC to minimize the impacts of a changing climate?

Objectives

CAP-BC, as a flexible systematic conservation planning tool, can provide a set of outcomes to support decisions of adapting our conservation plans to the projected impacts of climate change, thereby ensuring that our protected lands are as resilient as possible moving forward.

The tool is flexible to set different targets to the major conservation approaches (see Framework), set weights or hard constraint with some specific layers. This flexibility of the tool provide for diverse user groups a set of options to generate conservation solutions from a climate change perspective based on their needs.

This tool will be flexible for diverse user groups, preloaded with data, and updatable for future advances in BC climate change knowledge.

Data

A total of 878 layers are used for planning and running the different conservation strategies in the study area encompassed the province of British Columbia (BC), Canada. We generated 964,490 planning units that represent the land of the study area using a 1×1 km grid. Then, we collected and pre-processed all the data
